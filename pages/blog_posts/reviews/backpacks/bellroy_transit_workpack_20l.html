BELLROY TRANSIT WORKPACK 20L REVIEW CREATED ON: APRIL 30, 2024
INTRODUCTION

The Bellroy Transit Workpack (20L) may have been the first expensive backpack I have bought. I have quite a lot to say about it, but I will always remember it as the backpack that brought me into this expensive interest of backpacks.

The Bellroy Transpit Workpack starts at $199. It is marketed at 20 liters, has a clam-shell type design for the main compartment, and is pretty sleek with minimal compartments.

Why Did I Buy It?

I bought the backpack without actually giving it proper test run. Bellroy, for me, almost never misses when it comes to design. I wanted something minimal, and a backpack that I can both use as a travel backpack, but also something casual that I can use personally and for work.

The Pros

One of the pros of this backpack for me was the material quality. I went overseas with this backpack a couple of times and have taken it across the country and it has held up pretty well. I had no rips on the outershell, shoulder pads, or the elastic parts of the backpack.

Another pro, which is important to me, is that the backpack was fairly easy to clean. Light colored backpacks such as the one I owned can be prone to getting dirty. In my case, the backpack did not actually get all that dirty when it was worn around, put on the ground, and when taken on a hike. When it did get dirty, I just put it into the washing machine and it came out looking new.

The bag really does hold about 20 liters. What I like about this backpack is that it is kind of modular in the fact that you can really put anything you want in the main compartment. Whether you are packing clothes for a weekend trip, or bringing your gym clothes for after work -- it can hold quite a bit. When I put books, and my iPad in there, I never felt things shuffling inside the backpack either.

I am 50/50 on the water bottle slot. In one end it can hold my Yeti tumbler, in the other end, it protrudes inside the bag. That is sometimes the problem with these types of backpacks: you get a sleek profile when the water bottle slot is hidden, but you also lose some space in the interior.

The Cons

Oh man. There are honestly a lot of cons to this backpack. In the quickest way possible: it is not worth the $199 price tag.

This is a subjective opinion, but I actually do not really like the real-life design of this backpack. It looks off. I am not quite sure how to explain it -- but it never looks symmetrical. It looks wider than it should be, or slanted due to its baggy design.

I also have a big gripe with the shoulder straps which slip very often. Aside from the slippage which can cause shoulder pain, the shoulder pads hurt all together. For a $199 backpack, I expect the shoulder pads to be much more padded than they are.

The water bottle holder is ok. I actually like it when backpacks find clever ways to hide the water bottle holders. In this case, Bellroy allows you to use the water bottle slot as both a compartment slot and for your water bottle. Having said that, I have had multiple spills due to me clumsily forgetting to properly secure my bottle -- which means, I have had a fair share of spills. And those spills seep into the bag itself. I may be too strict on Bellroy here, but $199 is $199, and $199 is not cheap.

Speaking of $199, some of the best backpack makers who sell bags at this price offer great warranty. Companies like North Face, Osprey, and Timbuk2 all offer better warranty than Bellroy's 3-year warranty. It is a shame honestly. While I do not believe Bellroy uses cheap materials, at least in my experience everything was fine, you would expect a company like Bellroy who sells really expensive bags to offer a lifetime warranty on their products.
